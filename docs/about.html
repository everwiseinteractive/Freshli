The story of why we built Freshli, what drives us, and where we're going.

The Problem

Every year, one-third of all food produced globally is wasted — 1.3 billion tonnes thrown into landfills while 828 million people go hungry. In the UK alone, the average household throws away £700 of perfectly good food each year.

Food waste isn't just a financial problem. When food decomposes in landfill, it produces methane — a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than CO² over 20 years. If food waste were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases in the world, behind only the US and China.

The hardest part? Most of this waste happens at home. Not in farms. Not in supermarkets. In our fridges, our cupboards, our kitchens. We buy food with good intentions, life gets busy, and by the time we remember, it's too late.

"The best time to save a piece of food from the bin is the day before it expires. The second best time is right now."

Our Story

Freshli started with a simple frustration: opening the fridge on a Tuesday evening, seeing a bag of spinach you forgot about, and realising it's already past its best. That moment — repeated millions of times a day, in millions of kitchens — is where food waste begins.

We asked: what if your phone could warn you before that moment? What if it could suggest a recipe that uses the spinach, the half-pepper, and the eggs that are all expiring this week? What if it could do all of this without sending your grocery data to a server?

That's Freshli. An app that uses Apple's on-device intelligence to turn the contents of your fridge into meals, not waste. Private by design. Instant by nature. Useful by default.

Our Mission

To make food waste prevention feel effortless, rewarding, and communal — so that rescuing food becomes a habit as natural as recycling.

We believe the solution to food waste isn't guilt. It's not another documentary about landfills. It's making the right thing the easy thing. When your phone tells you what's about to go off, suggests a 15-minute recipe, and shows you that 347 other people just rescued food too — suddenly, saving that spinach feels like joining a movement, not doing a chore.
